Motion in a Plane

Motion in a Plane - Vectors

Position Vector

Definition: The position vector r of a particle in a two-dimensional plane is given by its coordinates (x, y) relative to an origin O.

Formula:

\[ \vec{r} = x \hat{i} + y \hat{j} \]

Displacement Vector

Definition: The displacement vector \( \Delta \vec{r} \) represents the change in position of a particle over time.

Formula:

\[ \Delta \vec{r} = (x_2 - x_1) \hat{i} + (y_2 - y_1) \hat{j} \]

Velocity Vector

Definition: The velocity vector \( \vec{v} \) is the rate of change of displacement with respect to time.

Formula:

\[ \vec{v} = \frac{d \vec{r}}{dt} = \frac{dx}{dt} \hat{i} + \frac{dy}{dt} \hat{j} \]

Acceleration Vector

Definition: The acceleration vector \( \vec{a} \) is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time.

Formula:

\[ \vec{a} = \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \hat{i} + \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} \hat{j} \]

Projectile Motion

Definition: Projectile motion is the motion of an object thrown into the air under the influence of gravity, following a curved trajectory.

Parameters Used:

  • θ (Theta): Angle of projection from the horizontal.
  • u: Initial velocity of the projectile.
  • uₓ (Horizontal Velocity): uₓ = u cos θ (Constant throughout motion).
  • uᵧ (Vertical Velocity): uᵧ = u sin θ (Changes due to gravity).
  • g: Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²).
  • T (Time of Flight): Total time the projectile remains in the air.
  • H (Maximum Height): The highest point reached by the projectile.
  • R (Horizontal Range): The total horizontal distance covered by the projectile.
  • x: Horizontal displacement at time t.
  • y: Vertical displacement at time t.
Time of Flight

Formula:

T = (2u sin θ) / g

Maximum Height

Formula:

H = (u² sin² θ) / (2g)

Horizontal Range

Formula:

R = (u² sin 2θ) / g

Equation of Trajectory

Equation of Motion:

y = x tan θ - (g x²) / (2 u² cos² θ)

Explanation: This equation represents the path of the projectile, which is a parabola.

Important Points in Projectile Motion
  • Maximum Range Condition: The range is maximum when the angle of projection is 45°. That is, Rmax occurs at θ = 45°.
  • Maximum Height Condition: The maximum height depends on the initial velocity and occurs at the peak of the trajectory.
  • Time to Reach Maximum Height: It takes half of the total time of flight to reach maximum height.
  • Symmetry of Motion: The path of the projectile is symmetrical. The time taken to rise is equal to the time taken to fall.
  • Velocity at Maximum Height: At the highest point, the vertical component of velocity is zero, but the horizontal component remains constant.
  • Nature of Path: The trajectory of a projectile is always a parabolic path (except for vertical motion).

Circular Motion

Definition: Circular motion is the movement of an object along the circumference of a circle or a circular path.

Angular Displacement: The angle through which an object moves on a circular path, typically measured in radians.

Angular Velocity: The rate at which an object rotates around a circular path. It is defined as the time rate of change of angular displacement, and is typically measured in radians per second (rad/s).

Formula: ω = dθ / dt

Angular Acceleration: The rate at which the angular velocity of an object changes with time, measured in radians per second squared (rad/s²).

Formula: α = dω / dt

Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)

In Uniform Circular Motion, the object moves in a circular path with a constant speed.

Key Characteristics:

  • Speed remains constant.
  • Velocity changes due to continuous change in direction.
  • Acceleration (Centripetal Acceleration) acts towards the center.

Formulas:

  • Centripetal Acceleration: ac = v² / r
  • Centripetal Force: Fc = (m v²) / r
  • Linear Velocity: v = rω
  • Linear Acceleration: a = rα
Non-Uniform Circular Motion (NUCM)

In Non-Uniform Circular Motion, the object moves in a circular path but its speed changes over time.

Key Characteristics:

  • Speed is not constant.
  • Velocity changes due to change in both magnitude and direction.
  • Has both radial (centripetal) and tangential acceleration.

Formulas:

  • Tangential Acceleration: at = d|v| / dt = rα
  • Net Acceleration: a = √(ac² + at²)

Kinematic Equations for Circular Motion

The kinematic equations for circular motion are as follows:

  • \( \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t \)
  • \( \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha \Delta\theta \)
  • \( \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^2 \)
Parameters Used:
  • θ: Angular displacement (in radians)
  • ω: Angular velocity (in radians per second)
  • ω₀: Initial angular velocity (in radians per second)
  • α: Angular acceleration (in radians per second squared)
  • t: Time (in seconds)
  • Δθ: Change in angular displacement (in radians)

Centripetal and Centrifugal Force

Centripetal Force: Centripetal force is the force that keeps an object moving in a circular path, always pointing towards the center of the circle.

Formula: \( F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} \), where:

  • m: Mass of the object (in kg)
  • v: Linear velocity of the object (in m/s)
  • r: Radius of the circular path (in meters)

Centrifugal Force: Centrifugal force is the apparent force that acts outward on an object moving around a center, perceived in a rotating reference frame.

Formula: \( F_{cf} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \), same as centripetal force but acts in the opposite direction (outwards).

Key Points:
  • Centripetal force is a real force that keeps an object in its circular path, while centrifugal force is a fictitious force that appears when observed from a rotating frame of reference.
  • Both forces have the same magnitude, but they act in opposite directions.

Circular Turning of Road & Bending of Cyclist

Circular Turning of Road:

When a vehicle turns along a curved road, the friction between the tires and the road provides the necessary centripetal force to maintain the curved motion. The coefficient of friction (\( \mu \)) between the tires and the road surface plays an important role in preventing the vehicle from skidding.

The frictional force (\( F_f \)) that provides the centripetal force is given by:

\[ F_f = \mu m g \] Where:
  • \( \mu \): Coefficient of friction between the tires and the road
  • g: Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)

The frictional force must be equal to or greater than the required centripetal force to ensure that the vehicle does not skid:

\[ F_f \geq F_c \] Simplifying: \[ \mu \geq \frac{v^2}{r g} \]
Bending of Cyclist:

When a cyclist turns in a circular path, they lean towards the inside of the turn to balance the centrifugal force. The friction between the tires and the road provides the necessary centripetal force to keep the cyclist in motion along the curve.

Formula for Centripetal Force in Bending of Cyclist: \( F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)

Formula for the Angle of Lean: The angle \( \theta \) at which the cyclist must lean can be calculated as:

\[ \tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{r g} \]

However, the frictional force here also plays a crucial role in maintaining the turn. The frictional force \( F_f \) between the cyclist's tires and the road is:

\[ F_f = \mu m g \]
Key Points:
  • The coefficient of friction between the road and tires plays a crucial role in both circular turning and the cyclist's bending motion.
  • In both cases, the frictional force must be sufficient to provide the necessary centripetal force to avoid skidding.

Conical Pendulum

Formulae for Conical Pendulum:

  • Vertical Component of Tension: \( T \cos(\theta) = mg \)
  • Horizontal Component of Tension: \( T \sin(\theta) = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)
  • Speed of the Bob: \( v = \sqrt{r g \tan(\theta)} \)
Parameters Used:
  • T: Tension in the string (in N)
  • θ: Angle between the string and the vertical (in radians)
  • m: Mass of the bob (in kg)
  • g: Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
  • v: Speed of the bob (in m/s)
  • r: Radius of the circular path (in meters)

Motion of a Particle in a Vertical Circle

Formulae for Motion of a Particle in a Vertical Circle:

  • Centripetal Force: \( F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)
  • Gravitational Force: \( F_g = mg \)
  • At the Highest Point: The velocity at the highest point \( v_{\text{min}} = \sqrt{g r} \)
  • At the Lowest Point: The velocity at the lowest point \( v_{\text{min}} = \sqrt{5 g r} \)
  • At the Horizontal Position: The velocity at the horizontal position \( v = \sqrt{3 g r} \)
  • Tension at the Highest Point: \( T_{\text{max}} = \frac{m v^2}{r} - mg \)
  • Tension at the Lowest Point: \( T_{\text{min}} = \frac{m v^2}{r} + mg \)
  • Tension at the Horizontal Position: \( T_{\text{horizontal}} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)
Parameters Used:
  • m: Mass of the particle (in kg)
  • v: Speed of the particle (in m/s)
  • r: Radius of the circular path (in meters)
  • g: Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
